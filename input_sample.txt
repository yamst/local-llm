The glass spires of Aethelgard did not simply pierce the clouds; they seemed to weave into them, drawing the moisture from the air to hydrate the hanging gardens that draped over the lower balconies like emerald velvet. Elias stood at the precipice of the 114th floor, his boots scuffing the reinforced carbon-fiber tiling. To his left, the automated transit drones hummed—a low-frequency vibration that one felt in the marrow of their bones rather than heard with their ears.

He pulled a slate from his pocket. The blueprints for the 'Resonance Core' were glowing a soft amber. It was a structure designed to harmonize with the wind, turning the brutalist gusts of the upper atmosphere into kinetic energy. It was efficient, it was beautiful, and according to the city council, it was entirely too expensive. But Elias didn't build for councils; he built for the ghosts of the old world, the ones who remembered when the sky wasn't a resource to be mined, but a ceiling to be admired.

Walking back into the pressurized atrium, the scent of recycled ozone and synthetic jasmine filled his lungs. The sensors adjusted the lighting to mimic a sunset that was currently obscured by a layer of industrial smog two miles thick. 'Elias,' a voice crackled over the intercom. It was Sarah, the lead structural engineer. 'The seismic dampeners on the south quadrant are reporting a three-percent variance. If we don't recalibrate before the monsoon hits, the whole cantilever is going to start singing like a tuning fork.'

'I'm on my way,' he replied, though his eyes remained fixed on the horizon. Out there, past the neon glare of the commercial districts, the wasteland was silent.
